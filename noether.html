Emmy Noether

1882, 1935 · The mathematician chemistry forgot

The Woman

1882, Born in Erlangen, Germany

Emmy Noether was born on March 23, 1882 in Erlangen, Bavaria. Her father Max Noether was a very respected mathematician. From an early age Emmy also seemed to be extremely talented, solving problems that others couldn't even comprehend. Despite this, she wasn't allowed to enrol in university just because she was a woman. She was only allowed to audit classes with each professor's personal permission. She seated in lectures she had no official right to attend. It was an early sign of the oppression she would face her entire life just for being a woman.

1907, PhD in Mathematics

In 1907 Noether earned her PHD from the University of Erlangen, graduating top of the class. She was one of the first women in Germany to achieve this, but unlike one would think the degree opened no doors. Universities would sill not hire women as professors, even if they were as distinguished as her. She spent years working without pay, substituting for her father when he was ill from time to time. She kept discovering groundbreaking mathematical concepts during this period of time, without any kind of recognition or pay.

1915, Göttingen University

In 1915 the infamous mathematicians David Hilbert and Felix Klein invited Noether to join them at the University of Göttingen, which was at the time the world's greatest center of mathematics. The faculty didn't want to allow it. Professors argued that soldiers returning from war would be humiliated to learn from a woman. Hilbert famously responded: "This is a university, not a bathhouse." She was eventually allowed to lecture, but only under Hilbert's name, and remained unpaid for a few more years.

1918, Her greatest theorem

In 1918 Noether published what is now known as Noether's theorem, a result so important that physicists and chemists still use it every day. It proved that every symmetry in nature corresponds to a conservation law. We saw this in our chemistry class. The theorem helped to unite different areas of physics and chemistry under a single idea. Despite its importance, Noether received almost no public credit at the time. The work was only praised by mathematicians but she was still ignored by the broader scientific world.

1933, Forced out

When the Nazi regime came to power in 1933, one of their first acts was to get rid of Jewish academics in German universities. Noether was dismissed that April. She had spent nearly two decades helping to build one of the world's greatest mathematical schools, mentoring hundreds of students who would then also shape the field. However,all of it was erased overnight. She had flee to the United States, taking a position at Bryn Mawr College in Pennsylvania which was one of the few institutions willing to hire her.

1935, Death

Emmy Noether died on April 14, 1935, just two years after arriving in America. She was only 53. The reason was complications from surgery to remove an ovarian cyst. Albert Einstein wrote her obituary in the New York Times, calling her the most significant creative mathematical genius that ever appeared on Earth. She died never having received the recognition her work deserved just because of her gender. Today her name is on theorems, rings, and mathematical structures used all over the world the world every single day.

The Science

⚗ Noether's Theorem, 1918

Noether's theorem is one of the most relevant in all of science. It says that every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conservation law. It basically means that if a system looks the same over a certain period of time, energy is conserved. If it looks the same in every direction, angular momentum is conserved. This we have seen in physics. Chemists use this everywhere, it explains why reactions obey energy conservation, why certain molecular transitions can't happen, and why the laws of chemistry are the same everywhere in the universe.

🔬 Molecular Symmetry

Noether's work on symmetry is the foundation of how chemists classify molecules. We have seen this in last unit. Every molecule belongs to a symmetry group, and that group determines almost everything about its chemistry, how it bonds, what reactions it can be part of, and what kind of light it absorbs. Water's bent shape at 104.5° never changes, it is a direct consequence of the symmetry rules that Noether's ideas describe. Without her framework, modern chemistry would have no actual foundation.

📡 Spectroscopy

Every time a chemist uses IR spectroscopy or NMR to identify a molecule they are applying selection rules that come from Noether's symmetry principles. These rules determine which molecular transitions are allowed and which are just can't happen when a molecule interacts with light. Without Noether's mathematical framework for symmetry, chemists would have no way to predict or even interpret spectroscopic data. Virtually every molecule ever identified in a lab has been confirmed using tools built based on her work.

⚛ Quantum Chemistry

Noether mainly worked with abstract algebra, her most famous concept being her theory of rings and ideals. This gave quantum mechanics its mathematical language. When chemists calculate molecular orbitals or electron behavior, they work in mathematical structures that Noether discovered. Her proof that polynomial rings are always finitely generated explains how quantum chemical calculations on molecules always converge to a finite answer, which is why computational chemistry is even possible. Every software package used to model molecules today runs on the algebra she invented.

🌍 Why it matters today

The reach of Noether's work extends far beyond chemistry. MRI machines work because of quantum mechanics built on her algebra. GPS satellites are based on conservation laws her theorems explain. Greenhouse gas science depends on the spectroscopic selection rules her symmetry laws provids. Quantum computing, the next form of technology, is entirely built on the mathematical concepts she developed. This all shows why she is one of the most influential scientists in history, and most people have never heard her name.
